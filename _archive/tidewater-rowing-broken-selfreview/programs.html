Programs Overview

Tidewater Rowing Club runs three concurrent tracks out of the same boathouse on Willoughby Bay. New rowers typically start in Learn-to-Row, then choose between Masters Rowing (fitness-focused, flexible) and the Competitive Team (structured training, regatta calendar). You can move between tracks as your goals change — plenty of our racers started as reluctant beginners, and plenty of our Masters rowers have raced before and are here to keep their stroke sharp without the training load of a full competitive season.

Learn-to-Row

A six-week guided introduction to sweep and sculling, all gear provided. No experience required.

Masters Rowing

Recreational and fitness rowing for adults 21+, with morning, evening, and weekend flexibility.

Competitive Team

Structured training and a full regatta calendar for rowers ready to race, from club sprints to nationals.

Learn-to-Row

Learn-to-Row is our on-ramp for total beginners and anyone returning to the sport after years away. Sessions run six weeks, twice a week, in stable, coach-accompanied launch boats before you ever set foot in a shell built for speed.

Curriculum

Week 1–2: land-based technique on ergometers, boat safety, and capsize drills in the pool. Week 3–4: your first strokes in a stable four-person shell with a coach aboard. Week 5–6: independent rowing in pairs and quads, and an intro to racing starts.

Gear Provided

The club supplies oars, shells, life jackets, and spray gear for every session. You'll want quick-dry athletic clothes and shoes that can get wet — everything else is on us for the full six weeks.

Safety First

Every outing includes a certified coxswain or coach in a motorized launch, a VHF radio check-in, and a hard stop on rowing if winds exceed 15 knots or visibility drops below a quarter mile. All new rowers complete a swim assessment before their first time on the water.

Cost: $225 for the six-week session, which includes USRowing membership and a Tidewater club singlet. Sessions start the first Saturday of April, June, and September. Contact us to reserve a seat — sessions cap at 16 rowers.

Masters Rowing

Masters Rowing is built for adults who want the meditative pull of a good stroke, a solid workout, and a crew that notices if you don't show up — without a race calendar dictating your Tuesdays. Most Masters rowers row two to four times a week, and many have full-time jobs, kids, or both.

Who It's For

Ages 21 and up, any fitness level. About a third of our current Masters roster came straight from Learn-to-Row; others rowed in college decades ago and are picking the oar back up.

Flexible Schedules

Choose a morning squad (6:00 AM, Tuesday/Thursday/Saturday) or an evening squad (6:00 PM, Monday/Wednesday). Row as many sessions as your week allows — there's no minimum attendance requirement.

Fitness, Not Pressure

Coaching focuses on technique refinement and steady aerobic work, with optional pieces for members who want to test themselves. A handful of Masters rowers race the Head of the Chesapeake each fall for fun, but it's entirely optional.

Competitive Team

The Competitive Team trains for regional and national regattas with a full periodized program: winter erg work, spring water training, and a summer race season. This track asks more of your calendar and your legs — and it delivers results.

Race Calendar

April: Tidewater Sprints (home regatta). May: Ocoee City Cup. June: Mid-Atlantic Masters/Open Regatta. August: Nationals Qualifier. October: Head of the Chesapeake. Full dates and entry deadlines are posted each January.

Selection

Boatings are set by coach evaluation using erg scores, seat racing, and technical assessment, reviewed every four weeks. There's no tryout to join the team — selection determines which boat you row, not whether you're welcome on the water.

Coaching & Commitment

Five water sessions a week (5:45–7:30 AM) plus one evening erg session, September through August. Expect real training volume: this track suits rowers chasing a specific competitive goal.

FAQ for New Rowers

Do I need to know how to swim?

Yes. All new rowers complete a basic swim assessment (treading water and a short swim in gear) before their first outing. It's a safety requirement, not a test of speed.
