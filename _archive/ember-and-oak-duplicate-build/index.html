The Oven, The Block, The Craft

Ember & Oak started with a single question: could a neighborhood pizzeria hold its own against the city's best without losing what makes it a neighborhood place? We built our answer around a white oak-fired oven imported from Naples, set it in a storefront on Maple Avenue that used to be a hardware store, and started baking.

The oven runs hot — north of 900°F — and every pie spends less than 90 seconds inside it. That heat is unforgiving, which is exactly the point: it rewards good dough, good tomatoes, and good judgment, and it hides nothing. Our dough proofs for 48 hours. Our cooks have burn scars they're proud of. Nothing about this is fast food, even though the fire is fast.

Six years in, we still close the kitchen for family meal at 4:30 every day, still buy our greens from the farm stand three blocks north, and still know most of our regulars by their usual order. That's the part we never wanted to lose.

Signature Pies

Four that define us — the rest of the lineup rotates with the seasons.

The Margherita Vera

San Marzano tomato, fior di latte, basil torn at the pass, a slow ribbon of olive oil. Simple enough to judge a kitchen by — and we're happy to be judged.

Nduja & Honey

Spreadable Calabrian sausage melts into the cheese, then gets cut with a drizzle of local wildflower honey and pickled fresno chiles. Sweet heat, built to linger.

Mushroom & Taleggio

Roasted maitake and cremini from Hollow Run Farm, taleggio, thyme, a whisper of truffle oil. Earthy, funky, the pie our farmers ask for by name.

The Oak Leaf

No sauce — just garlic-rubbed crust, mozzarella, shaved prosciutto laid on after the bake, arugula, lemon, and shards of aged parmesan. Bright and a little indulgent.

Ingredient Philosophy

We split our pantry between two places on purpose: Italy, for the two things Italy simply does better, and here, for everything else.

Our flour is milled in Naples from a blend developed for wood-fired ovens — soft, low-protein, built to stay tender under intense heat instead of turning tough. Our tomatoes are San Marzano D.O.P., grown in the volcanic soil of Mount Vesuvius and canned within hours of picking. We don't substitute either. There isn't a domestic equivalent worth the compromise.

Everything else comes from within a two-hour drive. Cheese from a creamery in Hollow Run. Greens, mushrooms, and stone fruit from farm stands we've bought from for years. Our menu changes with the seasons because the farms do, and because a tomato in August tastes nothing like one in February — we'd rather change the menu than pretend otherwise.
